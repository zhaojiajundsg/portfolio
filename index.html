Living Practice

Design as a way of living —
entangled with matter, season, and the unexpected.

My practice does not begin at the desk. It begins in the garden, at the workbench, in the moment a fox crosses the threshold and becomes a collaborator.

01 — Growing with Luffa

From seed to sponge:
a full cycle of material care

In a small London backyard, I planted loofah — luffa aegyptiaca — from seed. I germinated them in recycled plastic cups under the kitchen table, moved them outdoors in spring, built bamboo scaffolding by hand, added eggshell and kitchen compost to nourish the soil. I watched the tendrils grip, the flowers open and close, the fruit swell. When the season ended, what remained was not waste — it was a fibrous, biodegradable sponge. A material grown, not manufactured.

Growing something you will eventually use changes your relationship to it. You understand where it comes from, what conditions it needs, what it costs in time and attention. This is the foundation of what I understand as regenerative design: not just zero waste as a system principle, but care as a design method.

02 — A Fox, a Bag, and a Repair

When a non-human actor enters
the life of an object

I had made a leather crossbody bag by hand for my partner — saddle-stitched, hand-burnished, shaped across many evenings. One morning, a fox came into our garden and took it. When I found the bag, the shoulder strap had been partially eaten through.

I repaired it. I cut a new strap from black leather, stitched it to the original tan, and riveted the join. The bag now carries a visible record of this encounter — a seam where two leathers meet, a small interruption in the original design that is also its most interesting detail.

This experience sits at the heart of what I want to research: how non-human actors change the trajectory of designed objects, and what happens when repair becomes a design act rather than an afterthought.

Graduation Project

Thinking Aloud

Visualising the invisible labour of creative work — Royal College of Art, 2024

Design work is full of thinking that leaves no trace. Hours of research, observation, and synthesis — cognitive labour that is real and exhausting, yet invisible to colleagues, clients, and even to the designer themselves. This invisibility breeds anxiety.

"Not been able to see the output, feels like I didn't do much work."

— Junior designer interviewed during research, 3 years experience

Through six weeks of field research — interviews with designers at PA Consulting, ethnographic observation of design students, and a support group visit at Mind in Camden — I identified a core tension: cognitive labour is rendered invisible by its nature, and that invisibility is a primary driver of creative anxiety.

The key insight came from observation: designers unconsciously shake their legs, play with their hair, and tap their fingers when thinking hard. These subconscious behaviours are physical signatures of mental work. I designed two devices that capture these signatures and make them visible.

Device 1 — Thinking Aloud sits under the desk and senses leg movement. As the designer thinks, the motion pumps air through a tube, gradually inflating a red balloon on the desktop. The harder you think, the bigger it grows. Thought, made visible.

Device 2 collects sketches and paper records deposited by the designer and gives a thumbs-up — a simple, tangible acknowledgement that the work happened and mattered.

Selected Professional Work

GAO TEA — Brand & Packaging

GAO TEA

INDUSTRIAL DESIGNER — BRAND, PACKAGING, PROTOTYPE

A zero-waste tea brand built around Chinese tea paste — a solid concentrate that dissolves instantly in hot water, leaving no leaf residue. The design challenge was to create packaging that matched the product's ecological ambition.

The packaging is formed from dry-pressed moulded pulp — no glue, no complex structure, fully recyclable. A rotating dispensing lid lets users access tea paste without opening the container fully, preserving freshness and reducing exposure.

  • Reduce individual packaging without compromising moisture protection
  • Zero-waste structure — no adhesives, minimal material
  • Scalable container system — one lid fits multiple body sizes
  • Express the duality of convenient and traditional through form

The brand name GAO (膏) — meaning paste — shares its pronunciation with the Chinese word for "high quality." The logo fuses the Latin letters G, A, O with the Chinese character 膏, creating a mark that holds both traditions simultaneously.
