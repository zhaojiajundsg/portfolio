GAO TEA

A zero-waste tea brand built around Chinese tea paste —
and an experiment in turning its own by-product into packaging.

RoleIndustrial Designer
ScopeBrand · Packaging · Material R&D
MaterialTea-residue pulp
Year2025

A zero-waste tea brand built around Chinese tea paste — a solid concentrate that dissolves instantly in hot water, leaving no leaf residue in the cup. I designed the brand, the packaging, and a material experiment that turns the tea residue from production into the packaging itself.

The brand

GAO — 膏 — means paste in Chinese. We defined GAO as a convenient tea brand that simplifies brewing, offers authentic high-quality teas, and follows principles of sustainability and zero waste. Those three ideas — fast, authentic, zero-waste — turned out to map exactly onto the sound of the name:

  • G — Grab and go. A single tea paste dropped into water makes a cup of tea, simplifying the whole brewing ritual.
  • A — Authentic. Made from authentic Chinese tea leaves of the highest quality.
  • O — O waste. No residue and no waste generated in drinking it.

膏 means paste, while sharing its pronunciation with the Chinese word for “high quality.” It was this commitment to sustainability that led the project from a brand into a material experiment.

The challenge

The brief was to reduce packaging without losing the protection tea needs, and to express two qualities at once: the convenience of an instant product and the depth of a traditional one.

  • Reduce individual packaging without compromising moisture protection
  • Zero-waste structure — no adhesives, minimal material
  • Scalable container system — one lid fits multiple body sizes
  • Express the duality of convenient and traditional through form

The packaging

The packaging is formed from dry-pressed moulded pulp — no glue, no complex structure, fully recyclable. A rotating dispensing lid lets users access the tea paste without opening the container fully, preserving freshness and reducing exposure.

Making the tea paste leaves behind a by-product: spent tea leaves. Rather than discard them, I asked whether this residue could become the packaging itself — closing the loop so the waste from production becomes the material that protects the product.

From tea residue to pulp

To test the idea, I made paper from the tea residue by hand. The process: weigh out the tea leaves, brew them, and collect the spent residue left behind. The residue is then beaten down into a wet pulp, mixed, and finally couched and pressed into sheets — the traditional papermaking step of forming a sheet from suspended pulp.

Finding the right ratio

I then ran a series of tests, varying the proportions of tea residue, paper pulp, and starch paste. The tea-residue content ranged across 20%, 40%, 60% and 80%. The clearest finding: the higher the tea-residue content, the weaker and rougher the resulting sheet.

The best sheet came from a ratio of 20% tea residue, 60% paper, 20% starch paste — enough tea residue to keep the material’s identity and story, while staying strong and workable.

The prototype

Using the 20-60-20 pulp, I made a packaging prototype to test feasibility. A 3D-printed mould was used to wet-press the pulp into shape. The result confirmed the material works, but wet-pressing by hand could not match the precision and finish of industrial dry-pressing — the surface and wall thickness were less even.

The next step would be to work with a specialist moulded-pulp manufacturer to explore whether tea-residue pulp can be dry-pressed at production quality.
